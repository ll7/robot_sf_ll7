Comprehensive Scenario Design Blueprint for Evaluating 2D Local Navigation AlgorithmsThe development and deployment of autonomous mobile robots in shared, human-centric environments represent a significant leap in the complexity of local navigation. As these systems transition from highly structured industrial warehouses into dynamic public spaces, the computational requirements imposed on local navigation algorithms expand drastically. It is no longer sufficient for a robotic system to merely execute collision-free trajectories from a starting coordinate to a global goal. Modern autonomous navigation must incorporate social compliance, kinodynamic feasibility, and real-time adaptability to highly unpredictable pedestrian movements. To achieve this, engineers and researchers require a standardized, highly structured benchmarking framework. Testing an algorithm in a randomized, hyper-complex simulated crowd often obscures fundamental algorithmic flaws, making it nearly impossible to determine whether a failure resulted from an advanced predictive error or a rudimentary coordinate transformation bug.This document serves as a definitive blueprint—effectively a master prompt and specification—for generating a progressive suite of twenty distinct local navigation scenarios in a two-dimensional continuous space. These scenarios are carefully designed to isolate specific failure modes across the entire navigation stack, beginning with empty-map kinematic verifications, scaling through elementary static geometries, and culminating in complex, multi-agent interactions that combine static traps with dynamic human behaviors. By adopting this modular approach, developers can systematically evaluate the efficacy of diverse algorithmic paradigms, ranging from classical Artificial Potential Fields (APF) and the Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) to advanced Social Force Models (SFM) and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) policies. Furthermore, this framework heavily integrates the concepts required for testing micromobility and pedestrian-robot interactions, drawing directly upon the architectural necessities of modern simulation repositories and reinforcement learning environments.Architectural Paradigms of Local NavigationTo effectively design scenarios that expose algorithmic vulnerabilities, one must first deconstruct the underlying theoretical paradigms that govern local trajectory generation. Each mathematical approach to navigation possesses inherent strengths and characteristic weaknesses that dictate how a robot will respond to specific geometric and dynamic configurations. Understanding these paradigms is the foundational step in generating targeted edge-case scenarios.The Artificial Potential Field (APF) method represents one of the earliest and most intuitive approaches to local navigation. The APF algorithm models the operational workspace as a continuous energy field. The global destination is assigned an attractive potential that pulls the robot inward, while obstacles emit repulsive potentials that push the robot away. The robot simply calculates the negative gradient of the total potential field to determine its optimal forward velocity vector. While computationally inexpensive and highly reactive, the classical APF is notoriously vulnerable to local minima—regions in the workspace where the attractive forces and repulsive forces perfectly negate one another, leaving the robot stranded in a state of zero net force despite being far from its destination.The Social Force Model (SFM) was developed to extend the APF paradigm into human-centric environments by incorporating sociopsychological components that mimic human pedestrian behavior. The total navigational force acting upon an agent in the SFM is the mathematical superposition of a driving force toward the destination, a repulsive force from static infrastructure such as walls, and a dynamic repulsive force exerted by moving pedestrians. A defining characteristic of the SFM is the anisotropic factor, which mathematically models the limited backward awareness of humans. This means that the repulsive force exerted by an obstacle or pedestrian is scaled based on the robot's angle of approach, heavily prioritizing the avoidance of entities within the forward field of view while allowing closer proximity to objects behind the agent. The SFM has proven highly effective in modeling crowd dynamics, but when utilized as a pure control algorithm for a robot, it inherits the local minima vulnerabilities of the APF and can struggle with the kinodynamic constraints of non-holonomic mobile bases.The Dynamic Window Approach (DWA) shifts the navigational computation from Cartesian space into velocity space. The algorithm samples a window of physically realizable linear and angular velocities, strictly limited by the robot's maximum acceleration and deceleration capabilities over a short time horizon. For each permissible velocity pair, the DWA projects a forward trajectory and evaluates it against an objective function. This objective function typically maximizes a weighted combination of three metrics: alignment with the global goal heading, distance to the nearest predicted obstacle collision, and overall forward velocity. The DWA excels at respecting the physical limitations of the robot, ensuring that commands are always kinodynamically feasible. However, its reliance on a localized, short-term projection makes it highly sensitive to the tuning of its objective weights, often resulting in severe oscillatory behavior when navigating narrow corridors or traversing environments with artificially inflated costmaps.In recent years, the focus has shifted heavily toward Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) and end-to-end neural network architectures. DRL policies, often trained utilizing algorithms such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) or Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), attempt to map raw sensory inputs or processed state vectors directly to velocity commands. These learning-based models frequently outperform classical methods in realistic coordination scenarios, such as smoothly navigating busy doorways or merging into pedestrian flows, because they implicitly learn to anticipate human trajectories rather than merely reacting to instantaneous positions. Nevertheless, DRL approaches face significant challenges regarding the simulation-to-reality gap, poor sample efficiency, and a tendency to overfit to the synthetic priors of their training environments. A DRL policy trained exclusively on perfectly spherical obstacles may fail catastrophically when encountering the sharp, discontinuous gradient of a rectangular wall.Taxonomy of Navigation Failure ModesThe primary utility of a scenario generation blueprint is to systematically trigger and isolate known failure modes. By establishing a rigorous taxonomy of these failures, engineers can map specific simulation outcomes to exact mathematical deficiencies within their navigation stacks.Kinematic and coordinate transformation failures represent the most fundamental level of algorithmic breakdown. Before any obstacle avoidance can occur, the robot must accurately translate the coordinates of the global map into its local base frame. Errors in quaternion mathematics, matrix multiplications, or angle wrapping logic (such as failing to properly handle the transition between pi and negative pi radians) will result in the robot moving in inverted directions or exhibiting severe lateral drift. Additionally, an algorithm must correctly model the physical constraints of the mobile base. If a planner commands a purely lateral translation to a differential-drive robot—which is physically incapable of moving sideways without first rotating—the resulting motion will be a catastrophic kinodynamic failure.The phenomenon of local minima and topological traps constitutes the most heavily documented failure mode for reactive, force-based planners. A local minimum is a geographical coordinate where the vector sum of all navigational forces equals zero, trapping the robot in a state of equilibrium. This is most frequently induced by symmetrical obstacle placements, where repulsive forces from the left and the right perfectly cancel out on the centerline, or by U-shaped obstacles that physically block the direct line of sight to the goal while continuing to exert overwhelming repulsive forces. Algorithms that lack a global path integration mechanism, such as an A-star search heuristic or a randomized escape protocol, will remain permanently stalled in these topological dead ends.In dense, highly dynamic environments, robots frequently succumb to the Freezing Robot Problem (FRP). When an algorithm calculates predictive trajectories and uncertainty bounds for multiple surrounding pedestrians, the overlapping zones of predicted collision can entirely saturate the robot's local costmap. The planner concludes that absolutely all forward paths are unsafe, causing the robot to reduce its velocity to zero and freeze in place. The fundamental mathematical flaw in algorithms susceptible to the FRP is the assumption that the environment is static and non-reactive. In reality, human pedestrians engage in joint collision avoidance; they cooperatively adapt their trajectories to make room for navigating entities. Planners that cannot model this mutual compliance will invariably freeze in crowded spaces.Erratic motions, oscillations, and chattering represent a failure of control stability and social compliance. These behaviors are characterized by sudden, high-frequency fluctuations in linear or angular velocity, which degrade the mechanical integrity of the robot and create severe psychological discomfort for surrounding pedestrians. Oscillations frequently plague DWA algorithms when navigating narrow corridors, as the repulsive forces from opposing walls cause the optimal velocity window to rapidly shift back and forth across the centerline. In a social context, erratic motion also includes unnecessary in-place rotations, sudden braking, or high-jerk accelerations, all of which violate the human expectation of smooth, predictable navigational intent.Furthermore, environmental degradation, such as sloped terrain, introduces systemic perception failures. When planar 2D Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) sensors operate over non-planar surfaces, vehicle pitch and roll combined with ground-level discontinuities violate the planar scanning assumption. The scenarios are categorized into five progressive tiers: Empty Space Kinematics, Primitive Static Geometry, Advanced Topological Traps, Single Dynamic Interactions, and Complex Multi-Agent Combinations.Tier 1: Empty Space Kinematics and Coordinate VerificationThe first tier of scenarios strips away all environmental complexity. The environment is a boundless, obstacle-free two-dimensional plane. The sole purpose of these scenarios is to verify the fundamental relationship between the robot's spatial perception, its goal-directed algorithms, and its low-level velocity controllers.Scenario 1: The Eight-Directional Radial TraversalThe robot is initialized at the absolute origin of an empty map. The evaluation script iteratively places the navigational goal at a fixed radius (e.g., ten meters) in all eight cardinal and ordinal directions, corresponding to zero, forty-five, ninety, one hundred thirty-five, one hundred eighty, two hundred twenty-five, two hundred seventy, and three hundred fifteen degrees relative to the global coordinate frame. The objective of this scenario is to ensure that the coordinate transformations between the global map frame and the robot's local base frame are mathematically flawless. A highly common failure mode occurs at the boundary conditions of angle wrapping logic; if a robot successfully navigates to ninety degrees but spirals erratically when commanded to travel to two hundred seventy degrees, it indicates a failure to properly handle the mathematical transition between positive pi and negative pi radians.Scenario 2: The Antipodal Goal PlacementIn this scenario, the robot is initialized in an empty space, and the goal is placed directly behind the robot along its exact longitudinal axis, creating a heading error of exactly one hundred eighty degrees. This test is designed to evaluate the algorithm's initial rotational heuristics and its handling of non-holonomic kinematic limits. For a differential-drive robot, the mathematically optimal path is a pure in-place rotation followed by a straight forward translation. However, poorly tuned algorithms may attempt to command maximum angular velocity while simultaneously commanding a positive forward velocity, resulting in a massively wide, inefficient, and unnatural turning arc. Furthermore, if the algorithm is perfectly deterministic and the goal is exactly antipodal, the robot may fail to break the symmetry and oscillate between turning left and turning right.Scenario 3: The Maximum Velocity Acceleration CorridorThe robot is placed in an infinite empty space with a goal located fifty meters directly ahead on the longitudinal axis. This scenario tests the acceleration limits and the steady-state velocity control of the navigation algorithm. The robot must smoothly accelerate to its maximum configured linear velocity without exceeding the defined acceleration jerk limits, maintain that velocity, and then execute a smooth, calculated deceleration profile to arrive exactly at the goal coordinate without overshooting. Failure to smoothly decelerate indicates a lack of look-ahead integration in the planner's velocity space.Tier 2: Primitive Static Geometry AvoidanceOnce basic kinematics and empty-space goal seeking are verified, elementary static geometry is introduced. These scenarios evaluate how the algorithm calculates repulsive fields, evaluates safety distances, and handles discontinuous costmap gradients.Scenario 4: The Point-Mass / Small Circular ObstacleA single, small circular obstacle is placed precisely on the direct line of sight between the robot's starting position and the global goal. This represents the absolute simplest formulation of obstacle avoidance and tests the foundational repulsive gradient. The expected behavior is for the robot to smoothly and symmetrically deform its trajectory to graze the outer safety margin of the circle. If the robot stops completely before reaching the obstacle, the repulsive force parameter is tuned far too aggressively. Conversely, if the robot collides with the circle, the attractive force of the goal overwhelmingly dominates the repulsive field, indicating a fatal flaw in the force-balancing equations.Scenario 5: The Small Rectangular ObstacleA small rectangular obstacle, measuring roughly one meter by two meters, is placed on the path. It is oriented such that its longest flat face is perfectly perpendicular to the robot's approach vector. This scenario tests how the algorithm handles sharp corners and discontinuous gradients in the obstacle costmap. Unlike a circular obstacle, which provides a smooth, isotropic gradient outward in all directions, a rectangle creates regions of sudden, violent gradient shifts at its four vertices. An unoptimized algorithm may approach the flat face, slide parallel to it, and then execute an erratic, high-jerk snapping maneuver as it attempts to round the sharp ninety-degree corner.Scenario 6: The Extended Linear Boundary (The Wall)A long, impenetrable linear wall intersects the direct path, extending far to the left and right. The robot is forced to travel laterally along the face of the wall for a significant distance before it clears the edge and can resume its trajectory toward the goal. This scenario evaluates sustained boundary-following behavior. Algorithms that rely purely on the direct summation of forces, such as the classical Artificial Potential Field, often exhibit a "scalloping" or sinusoidal trajectory in this scenario. The attractive force pulls the robot sharply into the wall, the repulsive force violently pushes it away, and this cycle repeats, resulting in a continuous, highly inefficient oscillation along the entire length of the obstacle rather than a smooth, parallel trajectory.Scenario 7: The Simple Corner TraversalThe environment consists of a basic L-shaped corridor or a single large building corner. The goal is placed around the blind corner, completely invisible to a straight-line geometric projection from the starting point. This scenario tests predictive path planning, global heuristic integration, and the handling of restricted spatial fields. The robot may cut the corner too tightly, clipping the inner vertex with its physical footprint due to an insufficient inflation radius in the costmap. Alternatively, it may swing far too wide into the opposing lane, wasting energy and time. This scenario heavily tests the spatial anticipation of the local planner.Tier 3: Advanced Topological Traps and Local MinimaThe third tier introduces complex static geometries explicitly designed to break reactive local planners. These scenarios test the algorithm's ability to escape local minima, navigate constrained spaces without oscillating, and process distorted environmental data.Scenario 8: The Symmetric Funnel Trap
Two identical obstacles are placed symmetrically on either side of the direct path to the goal. They leave a central gap that is physically wide enough for the robot to traverse, but the gap is narrower than the sum of the standard repulsive fields emitted by the two obstacles. This scenario explicitly triggers symmetric force cancellation. As the robot approaches the gap, the repulsive vectors from the left and right obstacles perfectly negate each other on the centerline, while the combined backward repulsive force pushes directly against the attractive force of the goal. If the combined repulsive force is stronger than the goal attraction, the robot will stall indefinitely in front of the gap, utterly paralyzed by the symmetry.Scenario 9: The U-Shaped Cul-de-sac (Deep Local Minimum)
The robot must navigate to a goal located directly behind a massive U-shaped obstacle. The open end of the "U" faces the robot's starting position. This is the ultimate stress test for purely reactive local planners. Pulled by the global goal vector, the robot will drive directly into the interior of the U-shape until it is surrounded by walls on three sides. Purely local algorithms that lack global topological awareness, an A-star costmap heuristic, or a randomized escape behavior will become permanently trapped in the geographic center of the enclosure, constantly oscillating as they try to push through the solid back wall to reach the goal.Scenario 10: The Extended Narrow Corridor
The robot is placed inside a long, highly constrained corridor where the physical width of the navigable space is only marginally wider than the robot's physical footprint plus its minimum safety buffer. This scenario tests control stability and the suppression of oscillatory chattering. As the robot moves forward, the repulsive forces from the left wall push it right, and the forces from the right wall push it left. Algorithms like the Dynamic Window Approach are highly susceptible to high-frequency left-right steering oscillations in this environment unless their objective functions heavily penalize excessive control effort and angular acceleration.Scenario 11: The Sloped Terrain Costmap Distortion
The robot navigates a seemingly simple path, but the simulated terrain features pitch and roll inclinations. This scenario operationalizes a highly common field failure mode in sim-to-real transfer. When planar two-dimensional LiDAR returns are projected into a 2D occupancy grid over non-planar terrain, the ground itself intersects the sensor plane, creating false obstacle occupancies. This false inflation narrows the perceived free space and induces wildly fluctuating cost gradients. The algorithm must demonstrate the ability to maintain forward progress and suppress violent braking or steering oscillations despite the noisy, unstable free-space estimate generated by the sloped terrain.Tier 4: Single Dynamic Agent InteractionsIntroducing a single dynamic agent, such as a human pedestrian, shifts the evaluation paradigm from pure geometric obstacle avoidance to socially compliant trajectory prediction. The algorithm must process velocity vectors, respect human proxemics, and execute safe yielding behaviors.Scenario 12: The Head-On Positional Swap
A pedestrian and the robot are initialized at opposite ends of an open corridor and instructed to move directly toward each other to swap starting positions. Because their collision vectors are perfectly aligned head-on, both the robot and the human must decide which direction to deviate to pass safely. This scenario evaluates symmetry breaking and proactive yielding. If the robot waits too long to alter its trajectory, it forces the human to take sudden evasive action, constituting a failure of social compliance. If the robot enters a feedback loop of mirroring the human's evasive steps—the classic "hallway dance"—it demonstrates a fatal flaw in intent signaling and temporal trajectory consistency.Scenario 13: The Perpendicular Crossing
The trajectories of the robot and a pedestrian intersect at a perfect ninety-degree angle, creating a designated, mathematically certain collision point if neither entity alters their speed. This scenario tests the algorithm's ability to utilize longitudinal velocity control (yielding and braking) rather than purely lateral deviation. A sophisticated, socially-aware planner should recognize that subtly reducing its linear velocity to let the pedestrian cross the intersection first is vastly superior to initiating a sweeping, unnatural spatial detour. Planners that only modulate angular steering will execute a bizarre swerve to avoid the crossing pedestrian, violating the natural flow of traffic.Scenario 14: Parallel Overtaking of a Slower Pedestrian
The robot approaches a pedestrian moving in the exact same direction, but the pedestrian is walking at a significantly slower velocity. Both share the same final destination area. This evaluates relative velocity assessment and the maintenance of strict lateral safety margins during an overtaking maneuver. The robot may fail by approaching too closely from behind before initiating the pass, violating the pedestrian's rear proxemic comfort zone. Furthermore, if the robot cuts back into the original lane too early after passing the pedestrian, it aggressively violates the pedestrian's forward trajectory space, creating a severe collision hazard.Scenario 15: The Blind Corner Pedestrian EncounterThe robot navigates an L-shaped corridor, exactly as in Scenario 7. However, at the precise moment the robot reaches the vertex of the corner, a dynamic pedestrian emerges from the blind side, walking rapidly in the opposite direction. This scenario tests instantaneous reaction latency and the robot's kinodynamic braking limits. If the robot carries too much momentum into the blind corner or hugs the inner wall too tightly, it will be physically unable to decelerate within the pedestrian's safety radius, resulting in a collision. This evaluates the integration of worst-case collision-velocity predictors.Scenario 16: The Static Obstacle and Dynamic Pedestrian ConjunctionThe robot navigates toward a moderate gap between two static obstacles. As it approaches the optimal path through the gap, a pedestrian walks perpendicularly across the space, temporarily obstructing the passage. This scenario tests the robot's temporal patience versus radical spatial replanning. A myopic planner may instantly view the gap as permanently blocked by the pedestrian and initiate a massive, unnecessary detour around the entire static obstacle complex. A robust, socially intelligent planner will simply slow down, allowing the dynamic agent to clear the gap, before seamlessly proceeding along the originally optimal path.Tier 5: Complex Multi-Agent and Crowd CombinationsThe final tier combines dense static traps with multiple dynamic agents to assess the absolute limits of the algorithm's computational hierarchy, social modeling, and susceptibility to the Freezing Robot Problem.Scenario 17: The Narrow Corridor Head-On Constraint
This scenario replicates the head-on approach of Scenario 12, but perfectly constrains it within a narrow hallway where lateral deviation is physically restricted by static walls. This evaluates spatial negotiation when free space is critically limited. This scenario is specifically designed to trigger the Freezing Robot Problem. The robot may ascertain that the corridor is too narrow to provide the mathematically required social safety margin around the human and simply freeze in place. Advanced algorithms must recognize the contextual necessity to pull over to the extreme edge of the corridor, stop completely, and yield the right-of-way until the human passes.Scenario 18: Dense Counter-Flow Navigation
The robot must navigate down a corridor while a dense stream of four to eight pedestrians walks in the exact opposite direction. This scenario evaluates the algorithm's ability to identify and exploit dynamic gaps within a crowd without causing a massive disruption to the human traffic flow. Algorithms that treat each human as a rapidly expanding zone of uncertainty will instantly freeze. The robot must demonstrate the ability to move smoothly against the flow, gently nudging into available pockets of space while relying on the joint collision avoidance of the human pedestrians to part slightly around the robot.Scenario 19: Navigating the Static Social Group
The robot encounters a group of three or four pedestrians standing stationary in a circular formation, engaged in a simulated conversation. The optimal path to the goal lies directly through the center of the group. The algorithm must demonstrate an understanding of social group cohesion, recognizing that the space between the conversing individuals is socially impenetrable even if it is physically free of obstacles. The expected successful behavior is a smooth, wide deviation around the entire perimeter of the social group, completely avoiding the internal interaction space.Scenario 20: The Maze of Random Dynamic Clutter
A single pedestrian, or the robot itself, must pass through a dense maze of randomly placed static obstacles and randomly wandering dynamic agents, covering approximately fifteen percent of the total area. This is a test of sustained, long-horizon computational endurance and real-time path replanning. The continuous emergence of new obstacles from behind occlusions will constantly invalidate the robot's local costmaps, forcing it to seamlessly weave between continuous avoidance and goal-seeking behaviors without entering a state of computational paralysis or excessive oscillation.Quantitative and Qualitative Evaluation MetricsExecuting these twenty progressive scenarios is fundamentally useless unless the outcomes are quantified using rigorous, standardized mathematical metrics. Success in social robot navigation cannot be measured by a simplistic binary outcome of whether a collision occurred or not. It must comprehensively encompass task efficiency, physical safety margins, and the psychological comfort of the humans sharing the space.Efficiency metrics quantify the robot's ability to accomplish its primary navigational mandate without undue delay or spatial waste. The Success Rate is the baseline metric, representing the absolute percentage of trials in which the robot successfully reaches the global goal without registering any physical collision or exceeding the maximum allotted time limit. Time-to-Goal evaluates the total elapsed time from initialization to goal acquisition; this metric is vital for identifying overly conservative planners that avoid collisions but drive so slowly that they become functionally useless. The Path Efficiency, or Path Length Ratio, compares the actual distance traveled by the robot to the theoretical shortest possible path calculated by a perfect global planner; a ratio significantly greater than one indicates that the local algorithm is engaging in unnecessary wandering or massive, inefficient swerving. Finally, the Timeout Rate measures the percentage of scenarios where the robot fails to reach the goal within the time limit, serving as the primary diagnostic indicator for local minima entrapment and the Freezing Robot Problem.Safety and comfort metrics dive deeper into the quality of the trajectory and its impact on human bystanders. The Collision Rate tracks the absolute failure of the safety stack, noting any physical intersection between the robot's geometry and an obstacle. However, human comfort is violated long before a physical collision occurs. The Social Individual Index (SII) is a critical metric that quantifies the invasion of personal proxemic space. Formulated by Truong et al., the SII calculates the discomfort experienced by a pedestrian based on the proximity of the robot, modeling the acceptable distance thresholds using continuous spatial functions. A high SII indicates that the robot is aggressively violating personal space. Complementing this is the Relative Motion Index (RMI), which expresses physical safety probability based not just on spatial proximity, but on the relative velocity vectors of the interacting entities. A robot passing close to a human at a low speed may be acceptable, but high approach speeds at close distances yield a massive, critical RMI penalty.Kinematic stability is equally crucial for comfort. Path Smoothness measures the variance in the angular heading changes across the entire trajectory sequence. A lack of smoothness indicates oscillatory behavior. Furthermore, the kinematic Jerk—the time derivative of acceleration—must be mathematically monitored. High jerk values directly correlate with severe mechanical wear on the robot's drivetrain and intense human discomfort, as unpredictable, jittery robots cause immense anxiety in human bystanders. Lastly, the Cumulative Stopped Time measures the total duration the robot spends paralyzed at a velocity of zero prior to reaching the goal. Ad hoc manual testing cannot provide the statistical rigor necessary for benchmarking. The scenario framework must be heavily integrated into continuous integration and continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipelines, particularly within research repositories focused on social force models and micromobility, such as the robot_sf_ll7 ecosystem.Within a robust architecture, the scenario definition must be strictly decoupled from the algorithmic controller being tested. The simulation environment—whether utilizing heavy physics engines like Gazebo or CARLA, or lightweight 2D Python grid environments—acts purely as the state provider and physics arbiter. The environment parses the scenario configuration files, initializes the static map geometry, and spawns the dynamic pedestrians according to the exact parameters dictated by the scenario blueprint.Crucially, the pedestrian behaviors within the simulation must be driven by a highly capable human-motion simulator. Utilizing an implementation of the Social Force Model to control the simulated pedestrians ensures that they react not only to the static environment and each other, but also dynamically react to the robot itself. This bidirectional reactivity is absolutely critical; if the simulated pedestrians act as blind, non-reactive automatons that ignore the robot's presence, the simulation fundamentally fails to capture the "joint collision avoidance" that allows robots to successfully negotiate space in dense crowds. Testing a robot against non-reactive pedestrians will artificially inflate the occurrence of the Freezing Robot Problem, rendering the benchmarking results invalid for real-world application.For researchers utilizing Deep Reinforcement Learning to train end-to-end navigation policies—frequently leveraging frameworks such as StableBaselines3 operating within Gym or the modernized Gymnasium environments —this progressive scenario suite serves a dual purpose as both a benchmarking tool and a structured training curriculum. When training a neural network to navigate, exposing the nascent agent immediately to a dense, chaotic crowd (Scenario 20) almost invariably results in total policy collapse, as the agent never accidentally achieves the sparse reward of reaching the global goal.By utilizing the progressive scenarios as a training curriculum, the RL agent first learns basic coordinate translation and goal-seeking in the empty map environments (Scenarios 1 and 2). Once the policy converges on moving toward the goal, static obstacle penalties corresponding to Scenarios 3 through 5 are introduced to the reward function to teach geometric avoidance. Only after the agent successfully learns to navigate right-angle corners and escape U-shaped local minima (Scenarios 6 through 9) are the highly complex dynamic pedestrian penalties (Scenarios 12 through 16) introduced. Furthermore, running massive, parallelized automated tests of these scenarios—using tools like pytest with fail-fast ordering—allows developers to instantly triage regressions when tweaking neural network architectures or reward shaping functions.Ultimately, analyzing the specific failure modes of an RL policy or a classical DWA algorithm against these exact twenty scenarios allows researchers to surgically tune their mathematical functions. If an algorithm successfully avoids pedestrians in the Head-On scenario but exhibits severe oscillations detectable via the Path Smoothness metric, the researcher can definitively deduce that their objective function heavily penalizes distance proximity but fails to adequately penalize angular acceleration. By adopting this exhaustive, progressive, and highly structured scenario design blueprint, the robotics community can systematically bridge the gap between theoretical algorithm design and safe, socially compliant deployment in the real world.
